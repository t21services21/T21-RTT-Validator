Total questions: **15**  
Suggested pass mark: **12 / 15 (80%)**

## Section A – Multiple Choice (10 questions)

**Q1.** Which chart type is usually best for showing a **trend over time**?

A. Bar chart  
B. Line chart  
C. Pie chart  
D. Scatter plot

**Correct answer:** B

---

**Q2.** Which chart type is usually best for comparing **categories** (e.g.
revenue by region)?

A. Bar chart  
B. Line chart  
C. Histogram  
D. Scatter plot

**Correct answer:** A

---

**Q3.** Which chart type is usually best for showing a **relationship
between two numeric variables** (e.g. transactions vs sales)?

A. Bar chart  
B. Line chart  
C. Scatter plot  
D. Pie chart

**Correct answer:** C

---

**Q4.** What is one common problem with using **3D charts** in dashboards?

A. They always take longer to render.  
B. They often make values harder to read and compare accurately.  
C. They are not supported by any software.  
D. They cannot show more than two categories.

**Correct answer:** B

---

**Q5.** Why is it important to label axes and units clearly on a chart?

A. It makes the chart look more colourful.  
B. It reduces the file size.  
C. It helps viewers correctly understand what the numbers mean.  
D. It is only required for academic reports.

**Correct answer:** C

---

**Q6.** Which choice best describes a **good title** for a chart?

A. "Chart 1"  
B. "Sales"  
C. "Monthly sales by region, 2024"  
D. "Nice bar chart"

**Correct answer:** C

---

**Q7.** Why is it usually recommended that bar charts start their **y-axis
at zero**?

A. To make the chart look taller.  
B. To avoid exaggerating small differences between categories.  
C. To reduce calculation time.  
D. Because software does not allow any other option.

**Correct answer:** B

---

**Q8.** What is "chart junk"?

A. Old charts stored on disk.  
B. Unnecessary decorations (3D effects, heavy backgrounds, clip art) that do
   not help understanding.  
C. Charts that have bugs in the code.  
D. Charts that show more than one series.

**Correct answer:** B

---

**Q9.** Which of the following best describes **storytelling with data**?

A. Showing as many charts as possible on one slide.  
B. Using charts to support a clear narrative about what is happening and
   what actions to take.  
C. Using only text and no charts.  
D. Animating every chart.

**Correct answer:** B

---

**Q10.** In a presentation to senior leaders, which of the following is
usually most important?

A. Showing every code snippet used.  
B. Showing the largest number of complex charts.  
C. Highlighting a few key metrics and insights clearly.  
D. Speaking as fast as possible.

**Correct answer:** C

---

## Section B – Short Answer (5 questions)

**Q11.** Describe a situation where a **bar chart** would be more suitable
than a **line chart**.

**Model answer (guide):**  
Comparing total sales by product category in one month – categories are
separate groups, not a time series.

---

**Q12.** Give one example of a chart you might create for a UK or US
stakeholder using the retail or A/B test datasets from this pathway.

**Model answer (guide):**  
A bar chart of total revenue by region for UK stores, or a line chart of
conversion rate over time for the A/B test.

---

**Q13.** What three questions should a good narrative around a chart
usually answer?

**Model answer (guide):**  
1) What does this chart show? 2) Why does it matter? 3) What action or
next step should we consider?

---

**Q14.** How can too many colours or labels in one chart make it **harder**
for stakeholders to understand the message?

**Model answer (guide):**  
They create visual noise, making it difficult to focus on the important
patterns or comparisons.

---

**Q15.** In your own words, explain why **audience** matters when designing
charts and presentations.

**Model answer (guide):**  
Different audiences care about different levels of detail. Senior leaders
need clear headlines and key metrics, while technical teams may want more
method and context; designing with the audience in mind makes communication
more effective.
